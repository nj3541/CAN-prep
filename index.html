Module 1 – Circuit theorems

DC & AC steady‑state analysis with independent/dependent sources, superposition, source transformation, Thevenin/Norton, maximum power transfer, reciprocity.[web:157][web:160]

Core laws & elements

  • KCL: Algebraic sum of currents at a node is zero (entering = leaving).
  • KVL: Algebraic sum of voltages around a loop is zero.
  • Ohm’s law: \(V = IR\); for phasors in AC, \(\tilde{V} = \tilde{I} Z\).
  • Power: \(P = VI = I^2R = V^2/R\) (DC/instantaneous).

Source transformation

  • Series \(V_s, R_s\) ↔ parallel \(I_s = V_s/R_s, R_s\) for linear resistive networks.
  • Works for DC and phasor AC (include complex impedances).

Dependent sources

  • Keep dependent sources active in all theorems (do not “turn off”).
  • Control variable may be voltage or current elsewhere in the network.

Nodal & mesh – standard exam method

Nodal analysis

  1. Choose reference node (ground); assign node voltages to others.
  2. Apply KCL at each node (except reference) in terms of node voltages and admittances.
  3. For voltage source between node and reference → use supernode equation.
  4. Solve simultaneous equations for node voltages; then compute branch currents.

Mesh analysis

  1. Identify independent meshes; assign clockwise mesh currents.
  2. Apply KVL around each mesh using impedances and mesh currents.
  3. Current source common to two meshes → use supermesh and an extra constraint equation.
  4. Solve for mesh currents, then get branch currents and voltages.

Superposition & reciprocity

Superposition

  1. Keep one independent source active; turn off others (Vs→short, Is→open).
  2. Solve circuit for required response (voltage/current).
  3. Repeat for all independent sources; algebraically add individual responses.

Reciprocity theorem (for linear, bilateral networks)

  • In a single‑source network, current in branch 2 due to source in branch 1 equals current in branch 1 if the same source is placed in branch 2 (under suitable conditions).[web:157][web:179]
  • Exam type: prove reciprocity or use it to relate currents in different positions.

Thevenin, Norton & maximum power

Thevenin equivalent

  1. Remove load at terminals A–B.
  2. Find open‑circuit voltage \(V_{TH}\) at A–B (using nodal/mesh/theorems).
  3. Turn off all independent sources and find equivalent resistance seen at A–B to get \(R_{TH}\); include dependent sources properly.[web:157]
  4. Equivalent: source \(V_{TH}\) in series with \(R_{TH}\).

Norton equivalent

  1. Find short‑circuit current \(I_N\) between A–B.
  2. Use \(R_N = R_{TH}\); relation \(V_{TH} = I_N R_{TH}\).

Maximum power transfer

  • DC: \(R_L = R_{TH}\).
  • AC (complex): \(Z_L = Z_{TH}^*\) (complex conjugate).

Module 2 – Dynamic circuits

First and second order DC/AC transient analysis, complete response = natural + forced.[web:157][web:160]

First‑order RL / RC – complete response

General DE:

\( \dfrac{dx(t)}{dt} + \dfrac{1}{\tau}x(t) = \dfrac{1}{\tau}x_{\infty} \)

Solution:

\( x(t) = x_{\infty} + \big(x(0^+) - x_{\infty}\big)e^{-t/\tau} \)

  • RL: \(\tau = L/R_{eq}\) seen by inductor after switching.
  • RC: \(\tau = R_{eq}C\) seen by capacitor after switching.
  • \(x(0^+)\) from initial condition; \(x_{\infty}\) from steady‑state with sources.

Typical exam steps

  1. Find initial value \(i_L(0^-)\) or \(v_C(0^-)\) from pre‑switch steady state.
  2. Use continuity: \(i_L(0^+) = i_L(0^-)\), \(v_C(0^+)=v_C(0^-)\).
  3. Find \(R_{eq}\) seen by L/C after switching; compute \(\tau\).
  4. Find final value \(x_{\infty}\).
  5. Substitute into standard form and simplify.

Second‑order RLC – damping

Standard form:

\( s^2 + 2\alpha s + \omega_0^2 = 0 \)

  • \(\alpha = R/2L\), \(\omega_0 = 1/\sqrt{LC}\), \(\zeta = \alpha/\omega_0\).
  • Overdamped: \(\zeta > 1\), critically damped: \(\zeta = 1\), underdamped: \(\zeta < 1\).

Underdamped response

\( x(t) = e^{-\alpha t}\big(A\cos\omega_d t + B\sin\omega_d t\big) \)

\(\omega_d = \omega_0\sqrt{1-\zeta^2}\).

Exam hints

  • Be ready to compute \(\alpha, \omega_0, \zeta\) and decide case.
  • Use two initial conditions (value and derivative at \(t=0^+\)) to find A, B.

Module 3 – s‑domain & coupled circuits

Transformed circuits in s‑domain, mesh/node in s‑domain, transfer functions, poles/zeros, coupled coils.[web:157][web:177]

Transformed circuits in s‑domain

Impedances

  • R → \(Z_R = R\).
  • L → \(Z_L = sL\) (plus source if initial current ≠ 0).
  • C → \(Z_C = 1/(sC)\) (plus source if initial voltage ≠ 0).

Method (mesh/node in s‑domain)

  1. Draw s‑domain equivalent circuit with impedances.
  2. Include independent sources (DC → \(V/s\) or \(I/s\) step, etc.).
  3. Apply mesh or nodal analysis to get \(V(s)\), \(I(s)\).
  4. Express required variable and perform partial fraction expansion.
  5. Inverse Laplace to get time‑domain response.

Transfer function & poles/zeros

\( H(s) = \dfrac{Y(s)}{X(s)} \) where X(s) is input, Y(s) is output.[web:157][web:181]

  • Poles: roots of denominator → determine natural response & stability.
  • Zeros: roots of numerator → frequencies where output goes to zero.
  • Driving‑point function: input at a port / response at same port.
  • Transfer function: response at another port (voltage/current transfer, impedance, admittance).

Coupled circuits

  • Mutual inductance: \(M = k\sqrt{L_1L_2}\), \(0 \le k \le 1\).
  • Dot convention decides sign of mutual terms.
  • Use equivalent forms (T, Π, or ideal transformer model) when required.

Module 4 – Three‑phase & resonance

Complex power in sinusoidal steady state, 3‑wire and 4‑wire Y, unbalanced Δ, neutral shift, series/parallel resonance and plots.[web:157][web:159]

Complex power & PF

  • Phasors \(V, I\), angle φ (I lagging/leading V).
  • \(S = VI^* = P + jQ\).
  • \(P = VI\cos\phi\), \(Q = VI\sin\phi\), \(|S| = VI\).
  • Power factor: \(\cos\phi = P/|S|\) (lagging = inductive, leading = capacitive).[web:159]

Balanced 3‑phase

  • Y: \(V_L = \sqrt{3}V_{ph}\), \(I_L = I_{ph}\).
  • Δ: \(V_L = V_{ph}\), \(I_L = \sqrt{3}I_{ph}\).
  • Total power: \(P = \sqrt{3}V_L I_L \cos\phi\).

Unbalanced & resonance

Unbalanced 3‑phase

  • Unbalanced Y with neutral: treat each phase separately, then find neutral current.
  • Unbalanced 3‑wire Y and Δ: often use line equations or convert Δ↔Y for easier analysis.[web:157]
  • Neutral shift: occurs when neutral is floating and load is unbalanced.

Resonance in series RLC

  • Impedance: \(Z = R + j(\omega L - 1/(\omega C))\).
  • Resonant frequency: \(\omega_0 = 1/\sqrt{LC}\).
  • At resonance, current is maximum, circuit is resistive.
  • \(Q = \omega_0 L / R\), \(BW = \omega_0/Q\).
  • Plots: \( |Z|\), admittance, and phase vs frequency often asked qualitatively.[web:157][web:187]

Module 5 – Two‑port networks

Driving‑point & transfer functions, Z, Y, h, T parameters, conditions for symmetry/reciprocity, interconnections, T–Π transformation.[web:157][web:181]

Driving‑point & transfer functions

  • Driving‑point impedance: \(Z_{in}(s) = V_1(s)/I_1(s)\) at a port.
  • Driving‑point admittance: \(Y_{in}(s) = I_1(s)/V_1(s)\).
  • Voltage transfer ratio: \(G_v(s) = V_2(s)/V_1(s)\).
  • Current transfer ratio: \(G_i(s) = I_2(s)/I_1(s)\).[web:157]
  • Poles and zeros: from denominators and numerators of these functions.

Z & Y parameters (recap)

\(V_1 = Z_{11}I_1 + Z_{12}I_2\), \(V_2 = Z_{21}I_1 + Z_{22}I_2\).

\(I_1 = Y_{11}V_1 + Y_{12}V_2\), \(I_2 = Y_{21}V_1 + Y_{22}V_2\).

Conditions & interconnection (exam focus)

Conditions

  • Reciprocal (Z): \(Z_{12} = Z_{21}\).
  • Symmetric (Z): \(Z_{11} = Z_{22}\) and \(Z_{12} = Z_{21}\).
  • Similar conditions exist in Y, h, ABCD forms.[web:157]

ABCD (T) parameters

\(V_1 = AV_2 - BI_2\), \(I_1 = CV_2 - DI_2\).

  • Cascade of two‑ports → multiply ABCD matrices (A,B,C,D of overall = product of matrices).

Interconnections

  • Series connection → Z‑parameters add.
  • Parallel connection → Y‑parameters add.
  • Cascade connection → T (ABCD) parameters multiply.

Exam strategy (KTU EET201)

Align your revision with how the end‑semester exam is structured.[web:157][web:178]

Paper pattern & weightage

  • Part A: 10 × 3 marks (2 questions per module) – theory/short numericals.
  • Part B: 5 × 14 marks (1 from each module, with choices) – full problems.[web:157]
  • Most marks come from correctly solved Part‑B numerical questions.

Target per module

  • 1 theorem/concept question + 1 full numerical typical of that module.

3‑day crash plan

  • Day 1: M1 + M2 – solve at least: 1 nodal, 1 mesh, 1 Thevenin/Norton, 1 RL, 1 RC, 1 RLC damping case.
  • Day 2: M3 + M4 – 1 Laplace s‑domain problem, 1 coupled circuit, 2 three‑phase problems (balanced & unbalanced), 1 resonance question with Q & BW.[web:178][web:187]
  • Day 3: M5 – 2 two‑port parameter problems (Z/Y/h or ABCD) + solve 1–2 entire university question papers under time.[web:173][web:189]
